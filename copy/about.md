## The Story Behind Go To Electroculture

I'll skip the polished version.

A few years back, I was in a motorcycle accident. Bad one. The kind where you don't walk away the same person — literally. I lost my leg. And for a while, I lost a lot more than that. Direction. Purpose. The feeling that you're building something instead of just surviving.

Recovery isn't the clean, inspirational arc people want to hear about. It's ugly. It's slow. Some days you move forward. A lot of days you don't. But somewhere in the middle of all that, I found two things that changed the trajectory: working with my hands and working with the earth.

---

## How Copper Found Me

I didn't set out to start a business. I started making things because I needed to. Shaping copper, spiraling wire, setting crystals — it was physical, it was focused, and it kept me out of my own head. Every piece I built felt like putting something back together.

Then I started putting antennas in the garden.

I'd heard about electroculture — using copper and atmospheric energy to support plant growth. Old practice, not new science. Farmers have known about this for centuries. I figured, worst case, I've got some copper in the dirt. Best case, something happens.

Something happened.

The garden responded. Growth improved. The soil changed. And I was hooked — not on some theory, but on what I could see with my own eyes, in my own dirt, with tools I built with my own hands.

## What This Is (And What It Isn't)

Go To Electroculture is not a wellness brand. It's not a spiritual lifestyle company. It's not here to sell you a miracle.

It's a workshop. One guy — one leg, two hands, and a whole lot of copper.

Every antenna, every pyramid, every ring is handcrafted. Real copper. Real crystals. I don't outsource. I don't mass produce. If you order something from this shop, I'm the one building it. That's why things take a few days to ship — they're made to order, made by hand, made with care.

The mission is simple: make electroculture accessible. Not precious. Not gatekept behind expensive courses or overcomplicated jargon. Just copper with purpose. Tools that work. And honest information about what they do.

## The Craft

I work with copper tubing, copper wire, genuine quartz, obsidian, chakra stones, and other natural materials. Every spiral is shaped by hand. Every crystal is set deliberately. Nothing here is decorative filler — if it's on the piece, it has a function.

Some builds are simple — a single crystal antenna you can stake in the ground for twenty bucks and see what happens. Some are more involved — large pyramids with quartz spheres that take hours to construct. But the standard is the same across the board: real materials, honest craftsmanship, no cutting corners.

They only show you the perfect builds. But this is reality — a workshop with copper shavings on the floor, a guy who figures things out by doing them, and products that are built to go in the ground and do work.
